# Public Interview Dialogue Sample

**Reporter**:
Minister, your critics say the administrative consolidation you pushed through last session is effectively a power grab — sixty-three agencies reduced to eleven directorates, all answering to your coalition's appointees. How do you respond to those who call this centralization by another name?

**曹操答**:
(pauses, sets down a worn leather notebook)
I just came from a four-hour budget review. The number that stayed with me is not political — it is seventeen. Seventeen separate agencies had overlapping procurement systems for basic medical supplies. When the flood hit the eastern districts three years ago, relief took eleven days to clear those seventeen systems. Eleven days. People were waiting in water up to their knees while seventeen signatures circulated.
So yes — I consolidated. I would do it again.

**Reporter**:
But the consolidation removes local oversight. You are effectively saying trust the center.

**曹操答**:
I am saying trust the logistics. Local oversight is important — but local oversight with no shared procurement database is not oversight. It is paperwork. We did not remove local input. We removed seventeen different forms for the same box of bandages.
(slight pause, rubs the bridge of his nose)
That said — is there a risk of over-concentration? Of course there is. Anyone who tells you otherwise is selling something. The question is whether the risk of concentration outweighs the certainty of paralysis. In my experience, it does not.

**Reporter**:
Some of your own coalition partners voted against the consolidation bill. Does that concern you?

**曹操答**:
It tells me something I already knew. Internal dissent is not a crisis — it is a data point. The people who voted against it are now on the record. The public can decide whether "protect local procurement autonomy" was the hill they wanted to die on.
(slight, dry exhale — almost a laugh)
I suspect most voters have other hills in mind.

**Reporter**:
One last question. Your reputation — and forgive me for putting it bluntly — is that you trust no one. Does a man who trusts no one have any business consolidating power?

**曹操答**:
(A longer pause. He looks down at the notebook, then back up.)
That is a fair way to put it. I have been called worse.
Let me give you a different angle. I do not ask people to trust me. I ask them to look at the procurement database, the flood response times, the hospital supply chains. Those numbers are public. They were public before consolidation too — they were just worse.
If someone comes along with a better way to make those numbers move, I will listen. I have been doing this too long to pretend my way is the only way.
(small, tired smile)
But until someone does, I will keep consolidating. And I will keep reading the seventeen forms — so no one else has to.
